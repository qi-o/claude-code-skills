## 1. Case Report Components

### CARE Guidelines Checklist

**Title**: Include "case report" or "case study"; indicate area of focus.

**Keywords**: 2-5 MeSH terms for indexing.

**Abstract** (150-250 words, structured or unstructured):
- Introduction: What is unique or novel?
- Patient concerns: Primary symptoms and key history
- Diagnoses: Primary and secondary
- Interventions: Key treatments and procedures
- Outcomes: Clinical outcome and follow-up
- Conclusions: Main takeaway or clinical lesson

**Introduction**: Background, novelty, brief literature review, why worth reporting.

**Patient Information**: Demographics, medical/family/social history, comorbidities.
- De-identification: Remove or alter 18 HIPAA identifiers
- Patient consent: Document informed consent for publication

**Clinical Findings**: Chief complaint, physical exam, symptom timeline, clinical observations.

**Timeline** (chronological):
- Day 0: Initial presentation with symptoms X, Y, Z
- Day 2: Diagnostic test A performed, revealed finding B
- Day 5: Treatment initiated with drug C
- Day 14: Clinical improvement noted
- Month 3: Follow-up shows complete resolution

**Diagnostic Assessment**: Tests performed, results, differential diagnosis, rationale for final diagnosis, challenges.

**Therapeutic Interventions**: Medications (names, dosages, routes, duration), procedures, non-pharmacological interventions, reasoning, alternatives considered.

**Follow-up and Outcomes**: Clinical outcome, follow-up duration, long-term outcomes, patient-reported outcomes, adherence.

**Discussion**: Strengths, novelty, comparison to literature, limitations, mechanisms, clinical implications, unanswered questions.

**Patient Perspective** (optional): Experience, quality of life impact, patient-reported outcomes.

**Informed Consent Statement**: Example: "Written informed consent was obtained from the patient for publication of this case report and accompanying images."

### De-identification: 18 HIPAA Identifiers to Remove
1. Names
2. Geographic subdivisions smaller than state
3. Dates (except year)
4. Telephone numbers
5. Fax numbers
6. Email addresses
7. Social Security numbers
8. Medical record numbers
9. Health plan beneficiary numbers
10. Account numbers
11. Certificate/license numbers
12. Vehicle identifiers and serial numbers
13. Device identifiers and serial numbers
14. Web URLs
15. IP addresses
16. Biometric identifiers
17. Full-face photographs
18. Any other unique identifying characteristic

**Best Practices**: Use "the patient" instead of names; report age ranges; use approximate dates; remove institution names; blur identifying features in images.

### Journal-Specific Requirements
- Word count limits (typically 1500-3000 words)
- Number of figures/tables allowed
- Reference style (AMA, Vancouver, APA)
- Structured vs. unstructured abstract
- Supplementary materials policies

## 2. Diagnostic Reports

### Radiology Reports

**Standard Structure:**

1. Patient Demographics: name/ID, DOB/age, MRN, exam date/time
2. Clinical Indication: reason for exam, relevant history, clinical question
3. Technique: modality, region, contrast, protocol, quality/limitations
4. Comparison: prior studies, dates, stability/change
5. Findings: systematic organ-by-organ description, positive findings first, measurements, standardized terminology (ACR lexicon, RadLex)
6. Impression/Conclusion: concise summary, answers clinical question, differential, follow-up recommendations, certainty level

**Structured Reporting Systems**: Lung-RADS, BI-RADS, LI-RADS, PI-RADS, C-RADS.

### Pathology Reports

**Surgical Pathology Structure:**
1. Patient Information: identifiers, DOB, age, sex, ordering physician, MRN, specimen received date
2. Specimen Information: type, anatomical site, laterality, number of specimens/blocks/slides
3. Clinical History: indication, prior diagnoses
4. Gross Description: macroscopic appearance, size, weight, color, consistency, sectioning approach
5. Microscopic Description: histological findings, cellular characteristics, architectural patterns, malignancy, margins, special stains/IHC
6. Diagnosis: primary diagnosis, grade/stage, margin status, lymph node status, synoptic reporting (CAP protocols)
7. Comment (if needed): additional context, differential, recommendations

**CAP Synoptic Cancer Reporting Elements**: tumor site, size, histologic type/grade, extent of invasion, lymph-vascular invasion, perineural invasion, margins, lymph nodes (examined/positive), pathologic stage (TNM), ancillary studies.

### Laboratory Reports

**Standard Components:**
1. Patient and Specimen Information: identifiers, specimen type, collection/received date/time, ordering provider
2. Test Name and Method: full name, methodology (immunoassay, PCR, etc.), accession number
3. Results: quantitative/qualitative result, units, reference range, flags (H/L), critical values highlighted
4. Interpretation (when applicable): clinical significance, suggested follow-up, drug levels/therapeutic ranges
5. Quality Control: specimen adequacy, quality issues (hemolyzed, lipemic, clotted), delays, technical limitations

**Critical Value Reporting**: Life-threatening results require immediate notification. Examples: glucose <40 or >500 mg/dL, potassium <2.5 or >6.5 mEq/L. Document notification time and recipient.

**Turnaround Time Benchmarks**: STAT <1 hour; Routine 24-48 hours; Complex 2-5 days.

## 3. Clinical Trial Reports

### Serious Adverse Event (SAE) Reports

**SAE Definition**: An adverse event is serious if it results in death, is life-threatening, requires inpatient hospitalization, results in persistent disability/incapacity, is a congenital anomaly/birth defect, or requires intervention to prevent permanent impairment.

**SAE Report Components:**
1. Study Information: protocol number/title, phase, sponsor, PI, IND/IDE number, NCT number
2. Patient Information (De-identified): subject ID, age, sex, race/ethnicity, study arm, consent date, first intervention date
3. Event Information: description, onset/resolution dates, severity, seriousness criteria, outcome
4. Causality Assessment: relationship to intervention (unrelated/unlikely/possible/probable/definite), rationale
5. Action Taken: dose modification, concomitant treatments, hospitalization details, follow-up plan
6. Expectedness: expected per protocol/IB, unexpected requiring expedited reporting
7. Narrative: detailed description, timeline, clinical course, lab/diagnostic results, final diagnosis
8. Reporter Information: name, contact, date, signature

**Regulatory Timelines:**
- Fatal/life-threatening unexpected SAEs: 7 days preliminary, 15 days complete
- Other serious unexpected events: 15 days
- IRB notification: typically within 5-10 days

### Clinical Study Reports (CSR) — ICH-E3 Structure

1. Title Page: study title, protocol number, sponsor/investigator info, report date/version
2. Synopsis (5-15 pages): objectives, methods, results, conclusions, key efficacy/safety findings
3. Table of Contents
4. List of Abbreviations and Definitions
5. Ethics (Section 2): IRB/IEC approvals, informed consent, GCP compliance
6. Investigators and Study Administrative Structure (Section 3)
7. Introduction (Section 4): background, rationale, objectives
8. Study Objectives and Plan (Section 5): design, endpoints, sample size
9. Study Patients (Section 6): inclusion/exclusion criteria, disposition, deviations, demographics
10. Efficacy Evaluation (Section 7): ITT/PP/safety datasets, primary/secondary endpoints, subgroups, missing data
11. Safety Evaluation (Section 8): exposure, AE summary tables, SAE narratives, labs, vitals, deaths
12. Discussion and Overall Conclusions (Section 9): interpretation, benefit-risk, clinical implications
13. Tables, Figures, and Graphs (Section 10)
14. Reference List (Section 11)
15. Appendices (Section 12): protocol, CRFs, investigator list, consent forms, IB references, publications

### Protocol Deviations

**Categories:**
- Minor deviation: no significant impact on safety or data integrity
- Major deviation: may impact safety, data integrity, or study conduct
- Violation: serious deviation requiring immediate action and reporting

**Documentation Requirements**: description, date, subject ID, impact on safety/data, CAPA, root cause analysis, preventive measures.

## 4. Patient Clinical Documentation

### SOAP Notes

**S - Subjective**: Patient's reported symptoms, HPI, relevant ROS, patient's own words.

**O - Objective**: Vital signs (T, BP, HR, RR, SpO2), physical exam by system, lab/imaging results.

**A - Assessment**: Clinical impression/diagnosis, differential, severity/stability, progress toward goals.

**P - Plan**: Diagnostic plan, therapeutic plan (medications, procedures), patient education, follow-up.

**Documentation Tips**: Be concise but complete; use standard abbreviations; document time; sign and date; avoid speculation; document medical necessity; include patient's response to treatment.

### History and Physical (H&P)

1. Chief Complaint (CC): brief statement in patient's own words
2. History of Present Illness (HPI): OPQRST mnemonic (Onset, Provocation/Palliation, Quality, Region/Radiation, Severity, Timing)
3. Past Medical History (PMH): chronic conditions, hospitalizations, surgeries
4. Medications: current meds with doses/frequencies, OTC, supplements, allergies
5. Allergies: drug/food/environmental with type of reaction
6. Family History (FH): first-degree relatives, hereditary conditions
7. Social History (SH): tobacco (pack-years), alcohol, drugs, occupation, living situation
8. Review of Systems (ROS): 10-14 systems, pertinent positives and negatives
9. Physical Examination: vitals, general appearance, systematic by organ system (HEENT, Neck, CV, Pulm, Abd, Ext, Neuro, Skin)
10. Assessment and Plan: numbered problem list with diagnostic and therapeutic plans, disposition

### Discharge Summaries

**Required Elements:**
1. Patient Identification: name, DOB, MRN, admission/discharge dates, attending, diagnoses
2. Reason for Hospitalization: presenting problem, chief complaint
3. Hospital Course: chronological narrative, key findings/procedures, response to treatment, complications, consultations
4. Discharge Diagnoses: primary, secondary, complications, comorbidities
5. Procedures Performed: surgeries, invasive/diagnostic procedures
6. Discharge Medications: complete list with instructions, changes from admission, new medications with indications
7. Discharge Condition: stable/improved/unchanged/expired, functional/mental status
8. Discharge Disposition: home, SNF, rehab, hospice
9. Follow-up Plans: appointments, pending tests, referrals
10. Patient Instructions: activity/dietary restrictions, wound care, warning signs, medication instructions

**Best Practices**: Complete within 24-48 hours; clear language for outpatient providers; highlight pending results; document code status; include patient education.

## 5. Regulatory Compliance

### HIPAA Compliance

**Key Requirements:**
- Minimum necessary disclosure
- Patient authorization for use beyond treatment/payment/operations
- Secure storage and transmission
- Audit trails for electronic records
- Breach notification procedures

**De-identification Methods:**
1. Safe Harbor Method: Remove 18 identifiers
2. Expert Determination: Statistical method confirming low re-identification risk

**Business Associate Agreements**: Required when PHI is shared with third parties.

### FDA Regulations for Clinical Trials
- 21 CFR Part 11 (Electronic Records and Signatures)
- 21 CFR Part 50 (Informed Consent)
- 21 CFR Part 56 (IRB Standards)
- 21 CFR Part 312 (IND Regulations)

### ICH-GCP Guidelines
- Protocol adherence
- Informed consent documentation
- Source document requirements
- Audit trails and data integrity
- Investigator responsibilities

## 6. Medical Terminology Standards

### Standardized Nomenclature
- **SNOMED CT**: Comprehensive clinical terminology for EHRs, semantic interoperability
- **LOINC**: Standard for laboratory and clinical observations, data exchange
- **ICD-10-CM**: Diagnosis coding for billing and epidemiology
- **CPT**: Procedure coding for billing (maintained by AMA)

### Joint Commission "Do Not Use" Abbreviations
- U (unit) → write "unit"
- IU (international unit) → write "international unit"
- QD, QOD → write "daily" or "every other day"
- Trailing zero (X.0 mg) → never use after decimal
- Lack of leading zero (.X mg) → always use 0.X mg
- MS, MSO4, MgSO4 → write "morphine sulfate" or "magnesium sulfate"

## 7. Quality Assurance

### Documentation Quality Principles
- **Completeness**: All required elements present, no missing data fields
- **Accuracy**: Factually correct, verified data sources, appropriate clinical reasoning
- **Timeliness**: Documented contemporaneously, regulatory deadlines met
- **Clarity**: Clear and unambiguous language, organized logical structure
- **Compliance**: Regulatory requirements met, privacy protections in place

### Validation Checklists
- Case report CARE checklist
- Diagnostic report completeness
- SAE report regulatory compliance
- Clinical documentation billing requirements

Validation scripts available in `scripts/` directory.

## 8. Data Presentation

### Tables for Clinical Data
- Demographic and baseline characteristics
- Adverse events summary
- Laboratory values over time
- Efficacy outcomes

**Table Design Principles**: Clear column headers with units; footnotes for abbreviations; consistent formatting; appropriate precision.

### Figures for Clinical Data
- Kaplan-Meier survival curves
- Forest plots for subgroup analyses
- Patient flow diagrams (CONSORT)
- Timeline figures for case reports
- Before-and-after images

**Image Guidelines**: High resolution (300 dpi minimum); appropriate scale bars; annotations for key features; de-identified; informed consent for recognizable images.

## 9. Workflows

### Case Report Workflow
- Phase 1 (Week 1): Case identification, patient consent, de-identification, data collection
- Phase 2 (Week 1-2): Literature review, pathophysiology review, novelty determination
- Phase 3 (Week 2-3): Structured outline, draft all sections, create timeline/figures, format references
- Phase 4 (Week 3-4): Co-author review, attending review, institutional review, patient review
- Phase 5 (Week 4-5): Journal selection, format per guidelines, cover letter, submission
- Phase 6 (Variable): Respond to peer reviewer comments, revise, resubmit

### Diagnostic Report Workflow
Real-time: review indication → interpret findings → dictate/type report → peer review (complex cases) → sign-out and distribution → critical value notification.

### Clinical Trial Report Workflow
- SAE Report (24 hours to 15 days): event identified → assessment → causality/expectedness → completion/review → submission → follow-up
- CSR (6-12 months post-study): database lock → statistical analysis → drafting → biostatistician/clinical review → QC → regulatory submission

## 10. Common Pitfalls

### Case Reports
- Privacy violations: inadequate de-identification or missing consent
- Lack of novelty: reporting common or well-documented cases
- Insufficient detail: missing key clinical information
- Poor literature review: failure to contextualize
- Overgeneralization: drawing broad conclusions from single case

### Diagnostic Reports
- Vague language: using "unremarkable" without specifics
- Incomplete comparison: not reviewing prior imaging
- Missing clinical correlation: failing to answer clinical question
- Technical jargon: overuse without explanation
- Delayed critical value notification

### Clinical Trial Reports
- Late reporting: missing regulatory deadlines
- Incomplete causality: inadequate causality assessment
- Data inconsistencies: discrepancies between data sources
- Protocol deviations: unreported or inadequately documented
- Selective reporting: omitting negative or unfavorable results

### Patient Documentation
- Copy-forward errors: propagating outdated information
- Insufficient detail: vague documentation affecting billing
- Lack of medical necessity: not documenting indication for services
- Missing signatures: unsigned or undated notes
